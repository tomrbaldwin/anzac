The ANZAC's Long History of War Crimes and Impunity

The recent uncovering of serious war crimes committed by ANZAC soldiers abroad is not an isolated incident, but a long pattern of murder and impunity that goes back over a century.

25 April 2026

Investigations into ANZAC behaviour on the battlefield, especially in deployments in service of the United States’ war on terror, reveals a pattern of serious war crimes.

The Brereton Report, released in 2020 detailed the ANZAC’s practice of so-called blooding - the murder of unarmed captives as a initiation rite, and throwdowns - the practice of planting a weapon on their dead civilian victims so they appear to be combatants.

ABC’s investigation into the establishment of an interrogation facility in East Timor during the ANZAC’s deployment as a peacekeeping force

during the island’s tumultuous transition after decades of Australian Government policy backing Suharto’s brutal policy in Timor.

And now the criminal prosecution of ex-soldier Ben Roberts-Smith will hear testimony from witnesses to the murder of unarmed Afghanistan civilians at the hands of Australia’s most decorated soldier.

A theme in ANZAC battlefield investigations is the systemic cover up by soldiers, their code of silence tilting investigations to evade accountability.

This pattern of murder and coverups is not new to ANZAC behaviour on the battlefield.

Let’s take a look back to 1918 just one month after the end of World War 1.

ANZACs were camped in what is now known as Tzrifin, the eastern side of Rison LeZion, an Israeli Zionist colony in occupied Palestine just 40kms north of Gaza.

After World War 1, ANZAC soldiers were stationed across the region.

December 10, 1918

Surafend, Palestine

At midnight December 9th sergeant P was killed during an attempted robbery near the ANZAC encampment.

The killer was never seen, but P’s fellow soldiers suspected, without evidence, that they had fled to the nearby village of Surafend.

The following night hundreds of ANZACs encircled Surafend and ran riot through the village, bayonetting and beating to death any man they found.

They then set alight the homes of villagers and moved on to the next town.

Varying accounts put the number murdered between 20, and 137 according to a soldier who claimed to have counted the dead.

A subsequent investigation into the massacre ran into a wall of silence. Soldiers refused to participate and no one was held accountable.

Nearly 80 years after the massacre, Australian veteran Edward O’Brian, then 88, who was present at Surafen was recorded in an interview describing the massacre.

“They went through it with the bayonet” after “a good issue of rum” ”and they got it”.

In the same interview describing the indigenous population as ‘wicked … you would shoot them on sight’.

ANZACs were carrying a deeply held racism toward the indigenous populations back in Australia and New Zealand. Official creed to troops described the Indigenous Arabs as “lower in the human scale” and warned against feelings of empathy toward them.

The massacre in Surafen constitutes serious international war crimes and in the absence of accountability we have watched them repeated with alarming regularity over the last century.

ANZAC deployments have rarely serviced national sovereignty, instead serving foreign Empires in wars of aggression. Perhaps the only war for Australian sovereignty was fought by Indigenous people in the Frontier Wars against the British Empire’s colonial aggressors.

The lauding of the ANZAC as a personification of Australian values needs reevaluation if we truely seek high ideals worthy of a national character, rather than a face for nationalistic fervour.
