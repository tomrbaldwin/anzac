The ANZAC's Long History of War Crimes

The recent uncovering of serious war crimes committed by ANZAC soldiers abroad is not an isolated incident, but a long pattern of murder and impunity that goes back over a century.

25 April 2026

Since the first world war Australian government historians have used the ANZACs (Australian and New Zealand Army Corps) to build a national character, attributing them with high values, to create a nationalistic consciousness.

Recent investigations into ANZAC behaviour, especially in service of the United States’ war on terror, reveal a pattern of serious criminal conduct and impunity.

The 2020 Brereton Report details the ANZAC’s practices in Afghanistan of so-called blooding—the murder of unarmed captives as an initiation rite, and throwdowns—planting a weapon on their civilian victims so they appear to be combatants, echoing practices from Vietnam.

For decades, Australia’s support for the Indonesian occupation of East Timor helped secure access to Timor’s natural resources. In 1999 after Suharto's bloody campaign to influence Timor’s independence referendum, the ANZAC led INTERFET peacekeeping force was deployed to stabilise the country. An investigation revealed the ANZACs established an interrogation centre, which was used to interrogate mostly innocent civilians, including the torture of a disabled man with no connection to Indonesian paramilitaries. On the battlefield, witness testimony describes the mutilation of bodies by Australian soldiers.

In 2026 Ben Roberts-Smith, Australia’s most decorated soldier, the supposed personification of ANZAC values and the national character, has been charged with the murder of unarmed civilians in Afghanistan.

These investigations have all run into a systematic cover up by soldiers of all levels, the military’s code of silence so far evading accountability for their crimes.

This is a familiar theme of ANZAC behaviour.

December 1918

Just one month after the end of World War 1, allied soldiers were stationed across the region.

Surafen, Palestine

ANZAC soldiers were camped just outside Surafen, now known as Tzrifin, east of Rison LeZion, a Zionist colony in occupied Palestine, just 40kms north of Gaza.

December 9, 1918

At midnight December 9th New Zealand soldier Leslie Lowry was shot and killed during a robbery near the ANZAC encampment.

The killer was never seen, but Lowry’s fellow soldiers suspected, without evidence, that they had fled to the nearby village of Surafend.

The following night hundreds of ANZACs encircled Surafend and ran riot through the village, bayonetting and beating to death any man they found.

They set alight the homes of villagers before moving on to the next town.

Varying accounts put the number murdered between 20, and 137—according to a soldier who claimed to have counted the dead.

A subsequent investigation into the massacre ran into a wall of silence. Soldiers refused to participate and no one was held accountable.

‘They went through it with the bayonet’ after ‘a good issue of rum’.

Nearly 70 years after the massacre, Australian veteran Edward O’Brian who was present at Surafen, was recorded in an interview describing the massacre.

O'Brian said, ‘They went through it with the bayonet’ after ‘a good issue of rum’. In the same interview O'Brian described the Indigenous people of Palestine as ‘wicked … you would shoot them on sight’.

ANZACs held deep racism toward the Indigenous populations back in Australia and New Zealand, which they carried with them abroad. Official creed issued to troops described the Indigenous people of Palestine as “lower in the human scale” and warned against any feelings of empathy.

The massacre in Surafen constitutes serious international war crimes, and in the absence of accountability we have watched a shocking pattern emerge over the last century.

ANZAC deployments have rarely been in service of national sovereignty, instead serving foreign Empires in wars of aggression. Perhaps the only war fought in defence of sovereignty was fought by Indigenous people against the British Empire’s colonial aggression.

The lauding of the ANZAC as a personification of Australian values needs serious re-evaluation if we truely seek high ideals worthy of a national character, rather than a face for nationalistic fervour.
